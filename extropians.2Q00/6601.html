Joao's thesis is essentially that senescence must be due to a small set of
root causes because of the following empirical data:

(1) altering one or a few genes can markedly increase maximum lifespan, eg
SOD/catalase genes

(2) related taxa can differ markedly in maximum lifespan, eg, Pan & Homo;
Rivulus & Cynolebias; Salmo spp.

...but the logical fallacy here is plain: just because altering one
"controlling" parameter changes another system parameter markedly in one
direction; we cannot assume that the system has a tiny set of such
"controlling" parameters for that latter system parameter.

The empirical data that Joao cites certainly does not rule out his
hypothesis but it also does not lend any support whatsoever. It is logically
unrelated to his hypotheis.
************************
On the other hand, the assumption that metazoans are potentially infinite
lifespan entities in the absence of a few genetic faults is suspect on
biological grounds.

The genetic machinery necessary to produce the specialization & coordination
found in an advanced metazoan is enormous.

Such highly coordinated systems are especially failure-prone because of
their complexity. In vivisystems the naturally high-coordination,
decentralized "design paradigm" does allows graceful failure of an
individual system.

But that is all.

Robustness and immunity from failure are two quite different things.

To evolve a near-eternal organism in this scenario, the fitness of the
immortality phenotype would have to be truly enormous. The modifications to
the underlying genetics - to yield such a phenotype - would be truly
forbidding.

cymm
