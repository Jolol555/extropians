NASA's CASSINI MISSION TO SATURN IS SAFE

Dear Colleague:

Undoubtedly you have heard about NASA's upcoming Cassini mission to
Saturn, which is scheduled to be launched in October. It is an unmanned
probe that will explore Saturn and its mysterious moon, Titan, in
greater detail than ever before possible. Cassini is an international
project that is managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in California
and will be launched from Cape Canaveral, Florida, which is the in the
heart of my district.

Unfortunately, the news reports that you have heard about Cassini
probably did not focus on its ambitious scientific objectives, or the
international cooperation, or the engineering genius behind a probe that
will be operating nearly autonomously 1.4 billion kilometers away from
Earth. Instead, there are some fringe organizations that are opposing
the use of nuclear power for Cassini's on-board equipment.

Cassini does not use a nuclear reactor, but rather a set of special
generators that take the heat given off by the natural decay of small
pellets of plutonium-238 and convert it to electricity. This is similar
to the system used by previous deep-space probes. Additionally, all of
the radioactive material on board will be used essentially as batteries,
not for propulsion. The propulsion system is ordinary chemical rockets
that have been used for decades.

If there were any significant public threats posed by this mission, you
can be certain that I would be the first to protest it. I have family
and friends that live and work near the launch site, and I would never
allow their lives to be endangered.

There are many arguments used by Cassiniÿs opponents, all of which can
be quickly and accurately refuted by NASA. In fact, opponents have
planned events in Washington over the next few weeks to pressure elected
officials to oppose Cassini, and I would encourage you to get the facts
about the mission before drawing any premature conclusions. NASA's
Legislative Affairs office (358-1055) can provide you or your staff with
detailed data, or you can visit the Cassini World Wide Web site at
http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/cassini.

Cassini is an exciting deep-space exploration mission, and I hope you
will join me in supporting this important NASA program.

Sincerely,

<original signed by>

DAVE WELDON
Member of Congress

STOP CASSINI WEB SITE

NEWS MEDIA TOUR GUIDE

The STOP CASSINI WEB SITE was started in January, 1997 and has grown to
nearly 100 articles and newsletters, and numerous scanned GIF images of
NASA
documentation. It was created by computer programmer Russell Hoffman,
and
represents thousands of hours of research time and effort.

As word spread around the globe, the web site became noticed by the
pro-nuclear Cassini side and a number of debates ensued which were
carried
on in the web site's newsletter (now in its 37th issue). These debates
argue the logic of NASA's decisions regarding the use of plutonium "RTG"
power sources. In virtually all cases the pro-nuclear sides' arguments
are
presented in their entirety, including a long-running debate with Louis
Friedman, Executive Director of the Planetary Society, and with Jim
Spellman, President, California Space Development Council and Executive
Director, NSS/Western Spaceport Chapter, as well as many others.

The crux of the debate is not whether NASA is right or wrong or the
anti-nuclear Cassini people are right or wrong, because in fact, right
now
we have little way of telling if either side's "worst-case predictions"
are
correct! What is argued instead in the newsletters and at the web site
is:
First, that NASA cannot possibly KNOW FOR CERTAIN which of the many
statements which they present as fact, are in fact, facts. Second,
NASA's
own statements often are contradictory, misleading, or patently false
when
compared to their own literature. This is unscientific and profoundly
disturbing behavior on NASA's part.

The web site contains articles by Karl Grossman, Michio Kaku, Ross
McCluney,
Ross Wilcock, Perry Keidel, Chris Bryson, Michael Bein and others and
transcripts of speeches by Michio Kaku and Alan Kohn, as well as remarks
by
John Gofman, Ernest Sternglass, Jay Gould, Helen Caldicott, and Karl Z.
Morgan and others. Protest schedules and launch schedules are published
at
the web site, petitions are presented for signatures, and letters which
can
be sent to President Clinton, Al Gore, and others are also presented.
Much
of this material has since been republished elsewhere and some of the
material was originally published elsewhere, but most of the web site is
comprised of original essays published directly on the web. The
webmaster
admits to being outspoken, feisty, and passionate about the cause - and
occasionally wrong as well, but he has done his best to present
everything
he can about the issues involved.

In this printed book is a very small sample of the STOP CASSINI web site
-
about 5% of the total. The items include the official communication
with
NASA regarding their EIS reports. NASA has not officially commented on
the
web site to the webmaster, but he has heard from several sources that
they
have spoken disparagingly of it to others and are "reluctant" to appear
in a
debate with the webmaster. We are not, however, calling on NASA to
debate
or respond to the web site. We are calling for Congressional Hearings
so
that any and all statements made by both sides will be the sworn truth
as
they see it. At the same time, we call on all America, from schools and
colleges to workplace lunch cafeterias to CSPAN and all the news media
to
debate this issue. Not just Cassini, which is NASA's great new folly,
but
the entire cost of the nuclear nightmare the world faces on Earth and
now in
space as well.

At the end of the book is some information about the professional work
of
STOP CASSINI webmaster and newsletter editor, Russell D. Hoffman.
Although
Mr. Hoffman has reduced his workload in other projects this year to do
the
STOP CASSINI project (for the first time in about 18 years) he feels
that
the learning experience of how to use the World Wide Web to disseminate
information will result in better and more exciting future educational
projects for him to work on. The web is wonderful and must remain a
place
for the free and proper discourse of important public policy decisions
such
as CASSINI.

Sincerely,

Russell D. Hoffman
