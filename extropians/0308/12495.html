    Clearly we need more than energy to function. We need nutrients to
    build new protein and lipids for various functions, replacements for
    lost molecules and of course lots of water. We lose plenty of
    molecules and atoms because of the way we are evolved; it is easier
    to get replacements than recycle.

    A device that took ADP and phosphoric acid to ATP would need to be
    intracellular, since ATP is so quickly burned. A device that
    converted carbon dioxide and water to glucose would be more easily
    integrated, although it would have some cooling issues
    (energetically uphill, thermodynamic losses, likely at least a few
    tens of watts for normal metabolism). It would need to keep track of
    insulin levels in order not to cause type II diabetes.

    Using such a device in everyday life would be troublesome, since we
    can too easily get enough glucose anyway. The device might detect it
    and turn off (and even go into glucose burning mode), but it would
    not be much of an help. In an emergency it could provide energy, but
    there remains a need for protein, fats and some other essentials,
    and when you eat them you can just as easy get the calories.

    So in the end the device might be less useful than it seems. If it
    nanofactures other nutrients, e.g. from recycled waste, then it
    would be more useful.
