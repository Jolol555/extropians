    Thanks.

    These numbers are actually very interesting!

    To summarize:

    1) The researchers took measurements three times to compare the low-carb
    diet to the low-fat diet: at 3 months, at 6 months, and at 12 months.

    2) They measured ten different parameters:
            weight,
            systolic blood pressure,
            diastolic blood pressure,
            triglycerides,
            total cholesterol,
            LDL,
            HDL,
            AUC for glucose,
            AUC for insulin,
            insulin sensitivity

    3) Of the ten parameters measured, only the differences in ONE of them was
    statistically significant in all tests: HDL.

    HDL was better after the low-carb vs the low-fat diet at 3 months, at 6
    months and at 12 months. No other parameter was so clearly different from
    one diet to the other.

    As we've discussed here previously, serum levels of high-density lipoprotein
    (HDL) are inversely associated with risk of heart disease. The
    interpretation of the other nine parameters may be open to debate, but the
    low-carb diet shows a lower risk of heart disease at 3, 6 and 12 months.
    That much is not open to debate.

    You have argued that total cholesterol showed a tendency to increase on
    Atkins, but take a look at the numbers: they are totally irrelevant! The .1
    increase in total cholesterol after 12 months of Atkins is negligible. It is
    ridiculously insignificant, especially considering the 11 point increase in
    the healthy HDL form of cholesterol.

    -gts
