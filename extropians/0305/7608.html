    I wish I didn't agree with you. Gore would have been much more polite
    about things, but many of the contracts seem to have been let before the
    election was held, which makes one wonder slightly just how significant
    it would have been. My general impression is that the Democrats are
    more concerned with people liking them, and the Republicans are more
    concerned with shoring up their own power. Neither of those, however,
    translate directly into action, and there appears to be an underlying
    consensus between the parties as to the actions to be taken. One does
    wonder what the rationale is. The fact that it's kept secret does not
    automatically mean that it's something to be ashamed of, it might just
    mean that they don't believe that they could explain it in news-bytes,
    or that it depends on keeping it's future actions secret from other
    countries/power-groups. Still, one must bear in mind that a (small)
    group in control of power will almost always decide on actions that
    retain it's control over the power, however it may have to justify them
    to itself. Self-interest can warp ones decisions unseen, and will not
    usually be noticed by the beneficiaries, who will be righteously
    indignant over claims that they are corruptly self-interested. But how
    you evaluate what is in your self-interest will mould the final shape of
    the decisions.

    That said, Gore appeared from the first to be the "choosen looser" in
    the election (bland, unemotional, unable to motivate people). And he
    was as fervently in favor of increased centralized intelligence
    gathering and surveillance as Bush was. Perhaps more so. He was
    pushing that during Clinton's term. Still, I got the impression that
    everyone was surprised as how hard it was to get Bush choosen. (Why
    they wanted to is still a puzzle.)
