    --- Greg Jordan <jordan@chuma.cas.usf.edu> wrote:
    >
    > Hunting is a rather expensive hobby, actually, from what I have
    > heard. Where hunters are hunting, also, land cannot be used by
    > tourists observing wildlife or just hiking and camping out. That is
    > an economic loss in the recreational use of land.

    Camping out creates no economic activity beyond the purchase of
    gasoline. Every other expense incurred is equal to what one would spend
    to spend the weekend at home...

    A hunter goes beyond this:
    a) he buys a hunting license
    b) he buys a firearm
    c) he buys ammunition
    d) he takes a hunter safety course at least once in his life
    e) he expends ammo at the shooting range to practice and improve his
    accuracy, sight in his weapon, etc
    f) he joins a shooting club to perform (e)
    g) when he bags his game, he hires a butcher, and possibly a
    taxidermist.
    h) he joins an organization that engages in protecting and enhancing
    wildlife habitat.
    i) if he is traveling, he may hire a guide.
    j) if he is going to extreme wilderness, he may buy or rent other
    equipment like canoes, float planes, etc.
    k) he would also buy and/or rent all the same camping equipment that
    non-hunting campers use.

    Hunting finances all of the real game animal habitat preservation and
    enhancement that occurs in the US. You may think that the Sierra Club,
    or Earth First, or Greenpeace may do this, but they do not. Greenpeace
    only protects whales and dolphins and other marine mammals. Earth First
    protects trees, and the Sierra Club only protects miniscule and
    economically useless animals that reside in the way of massive
    commercial enterprises.
