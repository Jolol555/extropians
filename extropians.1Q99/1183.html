Since I share the libertarian/extropian/law-and-economics meme-set, I'm inclined to agree with this analysis, or at least wish it was true. However, to me, it is clearly an empirical question whether this actually is an accurate analysis.

The first thing that quite obviously sticks out to me is that the analysis assumes virtually non-existent transaction costs to information gathering and analysis. I have serious skepticism about ever acheiving this frictionless an economy in what I hope to be a extropian lifetime.

> > And, _off of the top of my head without deep analysis_, the coercive
> > imposition against all potential claimholders of limited liabilty
> > protection for stockholders does seem to be an actual market-enhancing
> > benefit provided by coercive governments.
>
> Market enhancing for who? It enhances the profit of the suppliers to the
> capital markets, but it costs the customers and employees of the corporations
> which borrow on the capital markets....its passing the buck downward.

I meant market-enhancing in reference to net externalities produced by statist coercion. I.e., that the beneficial externalities produced by encouraging large formations of limited-liability equity captial appear to outweigh the negative externalties imposed on society. "Market Enhancing," as an economic term, has nothing to do with transfer payments; it refers to correcting market failures that arise for various reasons.

(Just to forestall ideological objections -- I _do_ believe that government intervention in such market failures generally mucks things up more than it fixes them, due to human imperfection as well as rent-seeking behaviors, and therefore I'd prefer to live in a society where such intervention is kept to a minimum; however, I'm not ideologically blinded from the _possibility_ that such intervention might possibly be beneficial, and I'm trying to explore whether this might be such a case).
