Here is a table of some rough tensile strengths (real and theoretical) and
a list of my sources. I couldn't find anything on Aluminum Whiskers,
although I know I've seen a table on the Web that included them once.
IIRC, they have a higher tensile strength than carbon whiskers, but are
more than proportionately denser, making them an inferior choice for this
sort of application:

Todays Best Carbon Fiber: 6.9 Gpa [1]
Outer wall of Multi-walled BuckyTube: 63 Gpa [2]
Aluminum (cold formed) .36 Gpa 2,650 Kg/m^3 [3]
Steel (cold drawn) 1.24 Gpa 7,800 Kg/m^3 [3]
Titanium (cold formed) .93 Gpa 4,540 Kg/m^3 [3]
Graphite crystals 21 Gpa 2,200 Kg/m^3 [3]
Diamondoid 50 3,510 Kg/m^3 [3]
Thornel T-40 (Carbon Fiber) 5.65 Gpa 1,810 Kg/m^3 [4]
Spectra-2000 (plastic) 3.25 GPa 970 Kg/m^3 [4]
Steel 4.22 GPa 7,850 Kg/m^3 [4]
Theoretical Strength of Buckytubes : 200 Gpa [5]

[1] <http://home.att.net/~ederd/canon.html>
[2] <http://www.eurekalert.org/releases/wusm-mmm013100.html>
[3] <http://www.zyvex.com/nanotech/nano4/mckendreePaper.html#RTFToC7>
[4] <http://www.spacefuture.com/archive/space_elevators_space_hotels_
and_space_tourism.shtml>
[5] I've found many references claiming to have heard this number
    somewhere authoritive, but I've yet to find any authority who
    would claim this.

I should also mention that, according to [1], a reasonable space tether
design requires a minimum of 12.5 GPa tensile strength.
